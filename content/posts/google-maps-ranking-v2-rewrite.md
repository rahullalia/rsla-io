## What are the three ranking factors for Google Maps?

Google has publicly stated that Maps rankings depend on three factors. Understanding them is the difference between guessing and actually improving your position.

### Relevance

Relevance is how well your GBP matches what someone searches. If someone searches "hair salon" and your primary category is "Beauty salon," you're less relevant than the salon that picked "Hair salon" as their primary category.

This is why your primary category is the single most important optimization decision. Choose the most specific category available. "Mexican restaurant" beats "Restaurant." "Emergency plumber" beats "Plumber." Check what your top-ranking competitors use and match or beat their specificity.

You also boost relevance through your business description (750 characters, use your target keyword naturally in the first sentence), your services list (add every service you offer with descriptions), and your Google Posts (each post gives Google more context about what you do).

### Distance

Distance is how close your business is to the person searching. You can't change your physical location, but you can influence how Google calculates your service area.

If you're a storefront business, your address determines distance. Make sure it's accurate to the exact unit or suite number. If you're a service-area business (plumber, electrician, mobile detailer), set your service area in GBP to cover the cities and zip codes you actually serve.

One thing most businesses miss: Google weighs the searcher's location heavily. Someone searching from 2 blocks away will see different results than someone searching from 10 miles away. This means you can't just check your ranking from your office and assume that's what everyone sees. Use a local rank tracking tool like BrightLocal or LocalFalcon to see your rankings from multiple points across your service area.

### Prominence

Prominence is how well-known and trusted your business is online. Google measures this through:

- **Review count and rating:** More reviews with higher ratings signal trust. A business with 150 reviews at 4.6 stars will outrank a business with 12 reviews at 4.8 stars in most cases.
- **Review velocity:** Getting reviews consistently over time matters more than getting 50 reviews in one week (which looks suspicious).
- **Backlinks and citations:** Other websites mentioning your business (especially local news, directories, and industry sites) boost prominence.
- **Website authority:** Your website's overall SEO strength feeds into your Maps ranking. They're connected.
- **Engagement signals:** Click-through rate on your listing, calls made, direction requests, website visits from GBP. All of these tell Google that real people find your business useful.

## How do you optimize your Google Business Profile for Maps?

Your GBP is the foundation of everything. If your profile is incomplete or inaccurate, nothing else matters. Here's the full optimization checklist we run for every client.

### Nail your primary and secondary categories

This is step one because it has the biggest single impact on your ranking. Your primary category tells Google which searches to show you for.

Rules:
- Pick the most specific category that describes your core business
- Add 3 to 5 secondary categories for your other services
- Check your top 3 competitors' categories (search your main keyword, look at the map pack results)
- Revisit categories quarterly because Google adds new ones regularly

We've seen businesses jump 5 to 10 positions in Maps just by switching from a broad category to a specific one. Our pizza restaurant client went from "Italian restaurant" to "Pizza restaurant" and appeared in the map pack within 3 weeks.

### Write a keyword-rich business description

You get 750 characters. Most businesses leave this blank or stuff it with keywords. Both are wrong.

Write a natural description that includes your primary keyword in the first sentence, your city or service area, who you serve, and what makes you different.

Here's what we wrote for a contractor client: "Licensed general contractor in Sacramento specializing in kitchen remodels, bathroom renovations, and ADU construction. We've completed 200+ residential projects since 2015. Free estimates, transparent pricing, and a 5-year workmanship warranty on every job."

That's specific, human, and hits the target keyword ("general contractor in Sacramento") naturally.

### Complete every single field

Google uses profile completeness as a ranking signal. Every empty field is a missed opportunity. Go through and fill in:

- All services with descriptions and prices
- Products (if applicable)
- Attributes (wheelchair accessible, veteran-owned, women-owned, etc.)
- Opening date
- Social media links
- Business hours (including special hours for holidays)
- Payment methods accepted

A complete profile tells Google your business is legitimate, active, and worthy of showing to searchers.

## How do reviews affect your Google Maps ranking?

Reviews are the #1 local ranking factor according to multiple industry studies (Whitespark, BrightLocal, Moz). More reviews, higher ratings, and consistent review velocity all boost your position.

### Getting more reviews systematically

Don't leave reviews to chance. Build a system:

- Ask every satisfied customer in person, right after the service. "Would you mind leaving us a Google review? It really helps us out."
- Send automated review requests via SMS 1 hour after the appointment. We set this up through [GoHighLevel](https://rsla.io/blog/ai-marketing-stack-what-we-use) for every client. The timing matters because the experience is still fresh.
- Make it dead simple. Send a direct link to your review page. Go to your GBP, click "Ask for reviews," and copy that link.
- Follow up once if they don't review within 48 hours. Don't send more than one follow-up.

A contractor client went from 12 reviews to 47 in 60 days using automated SMS review requests. Their map pack ranking went from #7 to #2 in the same period.

### Responding to every review

Respond to every review within 24 hours. Every single one.

For positive reviews: Say thank you, mention something specific about their experience, and keep it genuine. "Thanks Sarah! Glad you loved the kitchen remodel. That tile backsplash turned out great."

For negative reviews: Acknowledge the issue, apologize, and offer to make it right offline. "We're sorry about the scheduling issue, Mark. That's not our standard. I've sent you a direct message to get this resolved." Never argue. Never get defensive. Future customers read your responses more carefully than the reviews themselves.

Google has confirmed that responding to reviews signals that your business is active and engaged, which feeds into prominence.

## What local SEO tactics improve Maps rankings?

Beyond GBP optimization, several off-profile tactics directly influence your Maps position.

### Build consistent citations

Citations are mentions of your business name, address, and phone number (NAP) on other websites. Consistency is critical. Your NAP must be identical everywhere: GBP, your website footer, Yelp, Yellow Pages, Facebook, BBB, industry directories.

Even small differences hurt. "123 Main St" vs "123 Main Street." "Suite 100" vs "Ste 100." "(555) 123-4567" vs "555-123-4567." Google sees these as conflicting signals.

Focus on 15 to 20 high-quality directories rather than blasting 200 low-quality ones. Start with: Yelp, Apple Maps, Bing Places, Facebook, BBB, your local Chamber of Commerce, and 2 to 3 industry-specific directories.

### Optimize your website for local SEO

Your website's local SEO directly feeds your Maps ranking. Key optimizations:

- Add your city and service area to your homepage title tag and H1
- Create individual location pages if you serve multiple areas (don't just list cities on one page)
- Embed a Google Map on your contact page
- Add LocalBusiness or Organization schema markup with your exact NAP
- Make sure your website NAP matches your GBP exactly
- Build internal links from your blog content to your service pages using local keywords

For a detailed walkthrough of local SEO, check our [complete SEO audit guide](https://rsla.io/blog/complete-seo-audit-guide-16-essential-steps-to-boost-your-rankings).

### Post Google Posts weekly

Google Posts are short updates (up to 1,500 characters) that appear on your profile. Think of them as social media for Google. Most businesses ignore them, which is exactly why doing them gives you an edge.

Post at least once per week. What to post:
- Offers and promotions ("20% off first service this month")
- Updates ("Now booking for summer projects")
- Tips related to your service
- Behind-the-scenes content (job site photos, team wins)

We schedule Google Posts through GoHighLevel's social planner so clients don't have to remember to do it manually.

### Get local backlinks

Backlinks from local websites are gold for Maps ranking. The best sources:

- Local news sites (pitch a story about a community project or milestone)
- Your Chamber of Commerce listing
- Local business associations and networking groups
- Sponsoring local events, sports teams, or charities
- Partner businesses (cross-promotions with complementary services)
- Guest posts on local blogs

One quality backlink from a local news site is worth more than 50 links from random directories.

## How do you track your Google Maps ranking?

You can't improve what you don't measure. GBP has built-in analytics, but you need a few more tools for the full picture.

### GBP Performance dashboard

Check these metrics monthly:
- **Search queries:** What keywords trigger your listing
- **Profile views:** How many people see your listing
- **Direction requests:** People asking for directions to you
- **Calls:** Direct calls from your listing
- **Website clicks:** Traffic from GBP to your site

### Local rank tracking tools

Because Maps results change based on the searcher's location, you need a tool that checks your ranking from multiple points:

- **BrightLocal:** Checks rankings from any location, tracks over time, generates client reports

## What mistakes tank your Google Maps ranking?

After optimizing dozens of local businesses, these are the mistakes we see most often:

- **Keyword-stuffing the business name.** Your GBP name must match your legal business name. "Joe's Plumbing" not "Joe's Plumbing Best Plumber in Denver Emergency Plumbing." Google will suspend your profile for this.
- **Inconsistent NAP across platforms.** Three different phone numbers on three different directories tells Google your data isn't trustworthy.
- **Ignoring negative reviews.** An unanswered negative review tells future customers you don't care. Respond professionally within 24 hours.
- **Using a virtual office or PO box.** Google penalizes businesses that don't have a real, staffable location (for storefront businesses). Service-area businesses should hide their address and set a service area instead.
- **Not posting Google Posts.** An inactive profile signals to Google that your business might not be operating. Post weekly.
- **Wrong business hours.** If someone shows up and you're closed, you'll get a 1-star review. Keep hours updated, especially for holidays and seasonal changes.
- **Buying fake reviews.** Google's detection has gotten aggressive. Fake reviews get removed, and your profile can be suspended. Build reviews legitimately through great service and a systematic ask process.
